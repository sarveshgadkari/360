Hearing Health Guide

Who needs a hearing aid?

Hearing aids work best for people with sensorineural hearing loss — the result of damage to the inner ear or auditory nerve. Common causes include:

  • Aging
  • Disease
  • Infection
  • Loud noises
  • Certain medications

Some patients with mixed or conductive hearing loss may also require a hearing aid after proper recommendation, following medication or surgical management.

Analog Hearing Aids

Amplify all sounds the same way; some store multiple program settings for different environments. Less common today.

Digital Hearing Aids

Convert sound to digital signals for exact duplication, background-noise handling, whistle reduction and flexible programming.

Rechargeable

No battery changes — hassle-free daily use without remembering battery-change dates.

Non-Rechargeable

Batteries changed periodically depending on model and hours of daily use.

Wearing Styles

Five types of hearing aids — one perfect fit for you.

BTE

Behind-the-Ear

For mild to profound hearing loss. Fits neatly behind your ear, attaching to a custom ear mold or thin tubing.

ITE

In-the-Ear

Fills the full or half shell of your ear. Best for those with dexterity issues; recommended for moderate to severe loss.

RITE

Receiver-in-the-Ear

Body sits behind the ear with a thin receiver wire into the canal — a more natural sound for mild to severe loss.

ITC

In-the-Canal

Fits deeper in the canal than ITE, so it's less visible. CIC (completely-in-canal) versions are nearly invisible.

CROS

CROS / BiCROS

For hearing in one ear only — a microphone on the poorer side routes sound to your better ear.

Buyer's Guide

How to choose a hearing aid — the honest way.

6 steps before you buy

  • Consult a qualified Audiologist (with RCI Registration)
  • Do your audiological evaluation
  • Take enough information regarding types of hearing aid
  • Ask the logical reason for the type recommended for you
  • Then go for a Hearing Aid Trial
  • If satisfied, take the decision to purchase

✓ Do's during trial

  • Compare your hearing with the aid switched off and on
  • Test in quiet rooms, rooms with noise, and outside traffic
  • Check your telephonic conversation
  • Check television sound if you require it
  • Compare at least two company models

✕ Don'ts during trial

  • Don't finalise on in-room communication only
  • Don't trial alone — bring someone who knows your hearing
  • Don't accept illogical counselling — ask till convinced
  • Avoid analog technology and below 6-channel digital aids
  • Don't buy without care & maintenance information
